Busby, Alberta. The proof.

Our Busby, Alberta facility is the operating heart of SkyFarms — 14 modular CEA units producing living lettuce, microgreens, and herbs for foodservice and retail partners. Every day, in every season, at consistent specification.

This is a SkyFarms operation — wholly operated, built, and managed by our team. Busby is where we proved the platform works: where the recipes are dialed in, the data layer matured, and the operating model that we'll deploy across Canada was stress-tested in real conditions. The crop quality consistency, the unit-economics math, the labour model — all of it was built here, with hands on the system.

Busby is the template. Every facility that follows — Abbotsford, Squamish, and the rest — runs the same modular Crop Motion architecture, the same operating playbook, the same yield specification.

Abbotsford, BC. The expansion.

Abbotsford sits in the Fraser Valley — the densest agricultural corridor in British Columbia and one of the most productive in Canada. The facility under acquisition adds 16 modular units to our deployed capacity, more than doubling the Busby footprint.

The strategic rationale: proximity to the Vancouver metropolitan area, established Fraser Valley logistics, and the regulatory familiarity that comes with operating in Canada's most agriculturally engaged province. Existing infrastructure means accelerated time to revenue versus a greenfield build.

The acquisition is in process. We will publish details upon close.

The Squamish Valley facility sits on land we acquired in 2021 — a former hops farm in the Sea-to-Sky corridor between Vancouver and Whistler. It will be the second SkyFarms facility, and the first to run end-to-end on BC Hydro hydroelectric power.

The site is currently in repermitting under an updated building design we're calling "vertical farming 2.0" — the lessons from Alberta operationalised, the system optimised for BC's climate, energy mix, and water profile. We're working transparently with the District of Squamish through the permitting process.

The facility will produce for the Sea-to-Sky corridor first — Pemberton, Whistler, Squamish, Vancouver — a market that today imports nearly all of its leafy greens from California and Arizona at the end of a 1,500-mile cold chain. We can be at the back of the restaurant before the lettuce is even refrigerated.

The Squamish facility is being developed in respect of the traditional territory of the Sḵwx̱wú7mesh (Squamish) Nation, and we are committed to meaningful community-benefits engagement throughout the build.
