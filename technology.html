— 01 / The growing system

One unit. One recipe.
One repeatable economic outcome.

A SkyFarms modular unit is a self-contained controlled-environment agriculture system. Every variable that affects plant growth — light spectrum, light duration, irrigation cycle, nutrient balance, water temperature, air temperature, relative humidity, CO₂ concentration, root-zone oxygen — is specified, monitored, and corrected continuously.

Each crop has a "recipe": the exact combination of inputs that produces the highest-yield, highest-quality output. Once a recipe is dialed in, it is repeatable across every unit we deploy. The same butterhead grown in our Alberta facility this week will be indistinguishable from the same butterhead grown in our Squamish facility next year.

This is the platform thesis. The growing system is the product; the produce is the proof.

— 02 / The science

Three variables. Engineered, not approximated.

— LIGHT

Spectrum, intensity, time.

Crops don't need "sunlight" — they need specific wavelengths at specific intensities for specific durations. Red light drives flowering and fruiting. Blue light drives vegetative growth and root development. Far-red and UV-A modulate everything in between.

Our LED arrays deliver crop-specific spectra at the precise PPFD (photosynthetic photon flux density) and DLI (daily light integral) each recipe requires. No clouds. No seasons. No waste.

— WATER

A closed loop. By design.

Field agriculture loses 95%+ of irrigation water to evaporation, percolation, and runoff. Our hydroponic loop recycles 98% of the water that touches the system — only the water that plants actually transpire out of their leaves leaves the unit.

Continuous filtration removes plant exudates and pathogens. Nutrient concentrations are corrected in real time. The same water that hydrates the seed hydrates the harvest two weeks later.

— CLIMATE

Specified, not weathered.

Temperature held to ±0.5°C. Relative humidity held to ±2%. CO₂ enriched to 800–1,200 ppm to accelerate photosynthesis. Air circulation engineered to maintain consistent leaf-surface boundary conditions.

The result: a growing environment that's the same in January as it is in July, the same in Alberta as it is in BC, the same on a Tuesday as it is on a Saturday. Variability — the enemy of every food-system supply chain — is removed by construction.

— 03 / The data layer

Every input measured. Every output known.

Every unit reports continuously: yield per tray, energy per kilogram, water consumption per cycle, light hours delivered, temperature variance, harvest count, days-to-harvest by crop. Our dashboard is the nervous system of the company.

When a unit's yield drifts from spec — a single tray underperforming by 8%, say — we know within hours, not at the next quarterly review. When energy consumption ticks up, we know it before the utility bill arrives. The same data layer that lets us catch problems lets us refine recipes, plan capacity, and price contracts with confidence.

This is the data infrastructure most CEA companies talk about and don't actually build. We built it because we have to: every shipping container we deploy is an independent profit center, and we need to know how each one is performing in real time.

— 04 / Why modular

The model that didn't burn $2.7 billion.

The vertical-farming sector's failures share a structure: monolithic warehouse facilities sized for venture-scale capital, requiring near-perfect utilisation to make the unit economics work. We do the opposite.
